Exploring the Relationship Between Educational Attainment and Unemployment Rates: A Regional Analysis of Industry-Specific Influences on Employment Outcomes

Research Introduction

Underemployment and unemployment remain constant challenges in the Philippines, significantly affecting both economic growth and individual well-being. Apart from reducing total household income, job insecurity has been shown to affect physical and mental health and social stability, creating ripple effects across communities (Wang et al., 2024). Filipinos often migrate abroad to secure employment, effectively relocating to another country just to find work. According to Rodriguez, Ang, Cerruti, & Opiniano (2024), in their Case Study on Skills Development for Filipino Migrant Workers, almost 10 million Filipinos work overseas, driven by limited domestic opportunities, and over 40% of newly hired OFWs in 2023 secured low-skill jobs.

Another major problem the Philippines faces is in education, where shortages in resources and persistent poverty limit opportunities for students. Recent reporting underscores the severity of infrastructure deficits in basic education. The Department of Education has acknowledged that the 165,000-classroom backlog in this country may take 55 years to resolve under current construction rates (Untalan, 2025).

These two issues are closely related. Educational attainment is a key factor in influencing job prospects, with higher levels of education generally improving employment opportunities, especially since many jobs require certain degrees for eligibility. Additionally, studies have shown that education can directly mitigate unemployment. For example, Mayombe (2021) and Mian et al. (2022), highlight that vocational programs and e-education initiatives, respectively, have improved employment rates.

Unemployment rates and educational attainment also vary across different regions in the Philippines. While some regions specialize in particular industries and others receive differing levels of resources for education and workforce development, it remains unclear how these factors shape unemployment outcomes. This project therefore seeks to examine the relationship between educational attainment and unemployment rates, and whether regional unemployment rates are influenced by the types of industries that dominate each region.

Problem

Despite the recognized role of education in improving employment opportunities, unemployment remains a persistent problem in the Philippines, and the exact relationship between educational attainment and unemployment rates has not been clearly established.

Solution

This project will investigate how levels of educational attainment relate to unemployment rates in the Philippines and whether industry composition plays a role, aiming to generate insights that can inform policies on education and employment.

Description of Data Set

PSA Labor Force Survey

Note that according to the latest PSA press release, data has been gathered up to July 2025. However, raw final datasets are only available online up to August 2024. Furthermore, datasets from April 2024 to August 2024 are missing some fields, such as Region and Major Industry Group, two very important fields needed to answer the research questions. The latest release with all necessary fields is from March 2024, hence that will be the one used for this study. All data fields of the Labor Force Survey take on numerical values. Also, data from Negros Island Region were collected as part of Western Visayas and Central Visayas.

Preprocessing:

  • From the Labor Force Survey dataset, 6 fields were taken: Region, C05 (Age), C07 (Highest Grade Completed), C11 (Location of Work), C15 (Major Industry Group), New Employment Criteria.
  • Entries with an empty employment status or "not part of the labor force" were ignored.
  • Employed and unemployed entries retained Region, Age, and Highest Grade Completed fields. Employed entries retained Location of Work and Major Industry Group. Unemployed entries had these set to 0.
  • Employment status was recoded: 1 for employed, 0 for unemployed.
  • Before preprocessing: 44,063 rows. After preprocessing: 20,518 rows.

Research Questions

Research Question 1

What is the relationship between the highest level of educational attainment (no educational attainment, primary, secondary, college, doctorate) and unemployment rates, and how does the degree of education influence the likelihood of being unemployed?

Research Question 2

Do different regions have significantly different unemployment rates due to prevalence of different industries per region?

Hypotheses

Research Question 1

Null: Educational attainment has no correlation with unemployment rates.

Alternative: Educational attainment strongly correlates with unemployment rates.

Research Question 2

Null: Different regions having different prevailing industries does not significantly affect differences in unemployment rates per region.

Alternative: Different regions having different prevailing industries significantly affect differences in unemployment rates per region.

RQ1: Effect of Educational Attainment on Unemployment

The logistic regression model predicts unemployment status based on education level with high accuracy (96.86%), indicating strong overall performance. The coefficient of 0.6169 for being unemployed suggests that the log-odds of unemployment increase with certain education levels. Translating this into an odds ratio of 1.8531 means individuals in these education categories are about 85% more likely to be unemployed compared to the reference group (likely "No Education"), which surprisingly shows the lowest predicted unemployment.

Based on the logistic regression analysis, educational attainment has a statistically significant effect on unemployment. The p-values for all education levels are less than the set alpha value 0.05, allowing us to reject the null hypothesis that education has no correlation with unemployment. This supports the alternative hypothesis that educational attainment strongly correlates with unemployment rates.

RQ2: Effect of Regional Industries on Unemployment

Based on the Chi-squared tests of independence, there is a statistically significant association among Region, Industry, and Employment status. The results show that the relationships between Region-Employment, Industry-Employment, and Region-Industry are all significant. Since the p-values for all tests are well below the alpha value of 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis and accept the alternative hypothesis. These results indicate that unemployment rates vary significantly across regions and that these variations are influenced by differences in industry composition within each region. Regions with different dominant industries show distinct unemployment patterns, suggesting that the prevalence of certain industries contributes to regional disparities in employment outcomes.
